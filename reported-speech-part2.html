Step 1
More Reporting Verbs — suggest, warn, accuse, explain
Four more verbs for your detective's notebook — each with its own pattern. Pay close attention: the preposition after accuse and warn is not optional.
SUGGEST  + -ing / that
Propose an idea or action
"Let's search the hotel records."
↓
Morrison suggested searching the hotel records.
Also: suggested (that) they should search… ✅
WARN  + obj + not to
Tell someone of danger or consequences
"Don't leave the city, Eleanor!"
↓
He warned Eleanor not to leave the city.
❌ He warned Eleanor to not leave… (wrong order)
ACCUSE  + obj + of + -ing
Say someone did something wrong
"You're lying, Webb!"
↓
Morrison accused Webb of lying.
❌ accused Webb to lie / that he lied
EXPLAIN  + that / to + obj
Give reasons or information
"The club is completely legal."
↓
Rosa explained (to Morrison) that the club was legal.
Note: explained to him (not "explained him")
📋 Full pattern summary — both parts
+ that claim · insist · explain · suggest + -ing deny · admit · suggest + obj + not to warn · tell · order · ask + obj + of + -ing accuse

Step 2
Reported Commands & Requests
Imperatives ("Do this! / Don't do that!") are reported using told / asked / ordered / warned + object + (not) to + infinitive. The word "don't" disappears entirely.
Positive:  told/asked/ordered + person + to + infinitive
Negative:  told/warned/ordered + person + not to + infinitive
Command → ordered
"Leave the room immediately!"
↓
Morrison ordered Webb to leave the room.
Request → asked
"Please tell me the truth."
↓
The detective asked Webb to tell the truth.
Neg. command → told + not to
"Don't touch the evidence!"
↓
Morrison told everyone not to touch the evidence.
Warning → warned + not to
"Don't contact Rosa Caruso!"
↓
He warned George not to contact Rosa Caruso.
⚠️ Never use "don't" in a reported command
❌ Morrison told her don't leave the city.
✅ Morrison told her not to leave the city.
⚠️ "not to" — word order matters
❌ He warned her to not leave the city.
✅ He warned her not to leave the city.

Step 3
Reported Questions — Yes / No
When the original question can be answered with yes or no, we report it using asked + if / whether. Tense backshifts. Word order becomes normal (subject + verb). No question mark.
asked + person + if / whether + subject + verb (normal order)  — no ? at the end
Three examples — spot the changes
"Are you hiding something?"
↓ asked + if + normal order + backshift
Morrison asked Webb if he was hiding something.
"Did you see Eleanor leave?"
↓ asked + if + normal order + backshift
She asked him if he had seen Eleanor leave.
"Can you explain the ransom note?"
↓ asked + whether + normal order + backshift
Morrison asked Rosa whether she could explain the ransom note.
if vs whether — both are correct
Both if and whether can be used for yes/no questions. Whether is slightly more formal — useful in press briefings and official reports.
He asked if she had left the hotel. ✅
He asked whether she had left the hotel. ✅
⚠️ Three things that must change
1 · No auxiliary  Did/Does/Is disappears from word order
2 · Normal order  subject + verb (not verb + subject)
3 · No ?  Reported questions never end with a question mark
❌ She asked if had he seen Eleanor?
✅ She asked if he had seen Eleanor.

Step 4
Reported Questions — Wh- Words
For questions with who, what, where, when, why, how, the wh-word stays, but word order changes to normal (subject + verb). No do/does/did. Tense backshifts.
asked + person + wh-word + subject + verb (normal order, backshift) — no ?
Four wh- examples from the case
"Where is Eleanor now?"
He asked where Eleanor was.
"Who sent the ransom note?"
Morrison asked who had sent the ransom note.
"What did you see at the bar?"
She asked Webb what he had seen at the bar.
"Why did Eleanor go to the Velvet Room?"
He asked why Eleanor had gone to the Velvet Room.
⚠️ The most common mistake
In a direct question, the verb comes before the subject. In a reported question, the order flips back to normal.
"Where is Eleanor?"  →  ❌ He asked where was Eleanor.
✅ He asked where Eleanor was.
"What did he see?"  →  ❌ She asked what did he see.
✅ She asked what he had seen.
Summary — two types of reported questions
Yes / No asked + if / whether + subject + verb
Wh- word asked + wh-word + subject + verb
Both: normal word order · tense backshifts · no question mark

Watch Out!
Common Errors — Part 2
Commands and questions produce their own errors. Spot what's wrong before clicking reveal.
Error 1
"don't" used in a reported command
❌ "Morrison told Eleanor don't contact anyone."
✅ Morrison told Eleanor not to contact anyone.
Reported commands never use "don't". Negative = not to + infinitive.
Error 2
accuse with wrong preposition
❌ "Morrison accused Webb to lie about the night."
✅ Morrison accused Webb of lying about that night.
Accuse always takes of + -ing. Never "to" + infinitive. Same with "suspect of".
Error 3
Question word order not changed
❌ "He asked where was Eleanor on the night of the 14th."
✅ He asked where Eleanor was on the night of the 14th.
In reported questions, word order returns to normal: subject + verb. Not the inverted question order.
Error 4
Question mark in a reported question
❌ "She asked if Webb had been to the Velvet Room?"
✅ She asked if Webb had been to the Velvet Room.
A reported question is not a real question — it's a statement that reports one. It never ends with a question mark.
